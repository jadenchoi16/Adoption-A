Regulatory Clarity Methodology

How the Regulatory Clarity Index is calculated , dimension ratings, scoring formula, tier thresholds, and primary data sources for 20 countries

Composite Formula

The Regulatory Clarity Index assesses how comprehensively a country has codified rules for blockchain and crypto activity across six distinct legal dimensions. Each dimension receives a rating of 1, 2, or 3. The composite score is the sum of all six ratings divided by the maximum possible score of 18.

Regulatory Score = (Stablecoin + Licensing + Tax + Banking + Enforcement + DeFi) / 18

This formula gives equal weight to each dimension (each contributes a maximum of 3/18 = 0.167). A country with all ratings of 3 scores 18/18 = 1.0. A country with all ratings of 1 scores 6/18 = 0.333 (Hostile floor). No country can score 0 under this model.

Rating Scale

1 · Absent / Hostile
No legislation exists, activity is banned, or the government has actively opposed this dimension. Provides no legal clarity or pathway.
2 · Partial / In Progress
Some rules exist or are under development, but gaps, ambiguities, or pending legislation leave significant uncertainty.
3 · Clear & Codified
Comprehensive legislation or formal guidance exists, actively enforced, with defined rights and obligations for participants.

Each of the six dimensions captures a distinct layer of crypto legal maturity. Below are the rating criteria and country-level assessments for each dimension.

Stablecoin Law

Legislation governing stablecoin issuance, reserve requirements, redemption rights, and authorized issuers.

3: Dedicated stablecoin statute (e.g., Japan PSA, UK DSB)
2: Covered under broader crypto or payments law
1: No specific legal recognition or banned
Rating 3: Japan  |  Rating 2: Ukraine, Brazil, Thailand, UK, Philippines, US, Turkey, South Korea, Nigeria  |  Rating 1: All others

Licensing Framework

Whether exchanges, custodians, and Virtual Asset Service Providers (VASPs) must register or obtain licenses.

3: Mandatory licensing with clear criteria & oversight
2: Registration required but framework incomplete
1: No formal licensing regime or prohibited
Rating 3: Japan, Ukraine, Brazil, Thailand, UK, Philippines, Turkey, Indonesia, South Korea  |  Rating 1: Bangladesh, Yemen, Ethiopia

Tax Clarity

Whether the tax authority has issued clear guidance on crypto gains, income, mining rewards, and staking income.

3: Formal guidance or legislation covering major event types
2: Partial guidance; some categories remain unclear
1: No guidance; treated as unregulated or banned
Rating 3: Japan, Ukraine, Brazil, Thailand, UK, US, Indonesia, India, Russia  |  Rating 1: Bangladesh, Yemen, Ethiopia

Banking Access

Whether licensed crypto businesses can open bank accounts and access payment rails without systematic de-banking.

3: Legally protected banking access for VASPs
2: Access in practice but no explicit legal right
1: Systemic de-banking or banking prohibited
Rating 3: Japan, Ukraine, Brazil, Thailand, Philippines, Nigeria, Turkey  |  Rating 1: Bangladesh, Yemen, Ethiopia

Enforcement Stance

Whether regulators actively enforce existing crypto rules and have taken meaningful action against non-compliant actors.

3: Active enforcement with track record of actions
2: Selective or developing enforcement capacity
1: No enforcement or enforcement used to suppress
Rating 3: Japan, Ukraine, Brazil, UK, Philippines, US, Argentina  |  Rating 1: Bangladesh, Yemen, Ethiopia

DeFi Recognition

Whether DeFi protocols, DAOs, or decentralized exchanges are acknowledged within the legal framework.

3: Explicit legal recognition or regulatory sandbox
2: Acknowledged in policy but not formally defined
1: Not recognized or implicitly prohibited
No country in the sample received a rating of 3. Rating 2: Japan, Ukraine, Brazil, Thailand, UK, US, South Korea  |  Rating 1: All others
Note on DeFi Recognition: The absence of any country scoring 3 on DeFi Recognition reflects the current state of global regulation , no jurisdiction has comprehensively codified DeFi protocol status, DAO legal personhood, and decentralized exchange obligations into a single, enforced framework as of early 2026. This is a known frontier that all countries are navigating. The EU's MiCA framework (effective Dec 2024) goes furthest but explicitly defers full DeFi coverage to future legislation.

Countries are grouped into four tiers based on their composite score. These thresholds reflect natural breaks in regulatory maturity rather than arbitrary cutpoints.

Key Observations

  • Japan leads (0.944): The only country in the sample with a dedicated stablecoin law (Payment Services Act revision), comprehensive VASP licensing, and full tax guidance. Japan's strict rules have also constrained adoption , it ranks 17th in economic adoption (0.284).
  • Regulatory paradox: The top 5 regulatory countries include three with relatively low economic adoption scores (Japan, Thailand, UK). Comprehensive regulation appears to slow consumer adoption while enabling institutional frameworks.
  • US classified as Developing (0.778): Despite being the largest crypto market, the US lacks a unified federal framework , rules vary by state and agency (SEC vs. CFTC jurisdiction dispute). No stablecoin law passed as of data collection.
  • Vietnam/Vietnam paradox: Vietnam ranks #1 in economic adoption but sits in the Ambiguous regulatory tier , the widest gap in the entire index between adoption and regulatory maturity.
  • All Hostile countries have near-zero adoption: Bangladesh, Yemen, and Ethiopia all score below 0.28 in economic adoption. No country with Hostile regulation has achieved meaningful blockchain economic penetration in this sample.

Rating Methodology

Ratings are assigned through a structured review of primary and secondary legal sources. The process for each country and dimension:

  • Primary source review: Check Library of Congress cryptocurrency regulation pages, national financial regulator websites (FSA, SEC, FCA, RBI, etc.), and official government announcements.
  • Secondary source cross-reference: Verify against PwC Global Crypto Regulation Report 2025 and Chainalysis Geography of Cryptocurrency Report.
  • Rating assignment: Apply the 1/2/3 rubric independently for each dimension based on whether clear, enforceable rules exist.
  • Conflict resolution: Where sources disagree, the more conservative (lower) rating is applied and noted.
Known data gaps: Ratings for Venezuela and Yemen are based on limited publicly available information. Venezuela's regulatory environment is particularly volatile and may lag real conditions. Yemen's regulatory situation is complicated by the ongoing conflict limiting government function. These are marked as lower-confidence entries. Pakistan's framework changed rapidly in 2025–2026 with the establishment of the Pakistan Digital Asset Authority (PDAA); ratings reflect the pre-PDAA baseline and may understate current progress.

Key Limitations

  • Ordinal ratings are subjective: The 1/2/3 rubric requires judgment calls at the 1/2 and 2/3 boundaries. Two analysts might rate the same country's licensing framework differently based on interpretation of "comprehensive." Inter-rater reliability has not been formally tested.
  • Point-in-time snapshot: Regulatory environments change rapidly. Several countries had major legislative changes in 2025 (EU MiCA, UAE VARA, Pakistan PDAA). Ratings reflect the state at data collection and may be quickly outdated.
  • No DeFi 3 rating possible (current state): The absence of any country achieving 3 on DeFi Recognition is a real constraint of the current regulatory landscape, not a modeling artifact. This will change as MiCA implementation progresses.
  • Enforcement quality not captured: A country with a rating of 3 on Enforcement may be enforcing regulations aggressively against consumers (bad) or against fraud (good). The rating measures existence and activity of enforcement, not its quality or market-friendliness.
  • 20-country sample bias: The sample overrepresents countries with significant crypto activity. Countries with neither activity nor regulation (most of sub-Saharan Africa, Central Asia) are not represented and would largely score as Hostile.
  • Federal vs. sub-national variation: For federal systems (US, India, Brazil, Australia), ratings reflect the national framework. Significant state/provincial variation may exist, e.g., Wyoming (US) has more progressive crypto laws than the federal baseline.
