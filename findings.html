Key Findings

What the data actually says about regulation and adoption.

Across 20 countries, the same regulatory tools produced opposite outcomes. The pattern only becomes visible when regulation is read against a country’s underlying economic conditions.

March 2026 · 20 countries · 3 dimensions
The Headline Finding

The relationship between regulation and crypto adoption is conditional, not uniform.

The same restrictive law produces opposite outcomes in different countries depending on the economic conditions underneath it.

In stable economies
Restriction suppresses adoption.
When citizens have functional banks, stable currency, and financial access, regulatory friction does what it’s designed to do.
In distressed economies
Restriction accelerates independent, decentralized adoption.
When the financial system is failing, restriction pushes demand into P2P, underground, and self-custodial channels—outside any regulator’s reach.
The Japan Paradox

Comprehensive regulation does not produce adoption when there is no economic need.

Japan has the highest Regulatory Clarity score in the index but ranks 18th in Economic Adoption. It is the single clearest piece of evidence that “more regulation = more adoption” is wrong.

Nigeria vs. Egypt

Same regulatory tool. Opposite outcomes.

Two countries, two restrictive policies, two completely different trajectories. The regulation isn’t the variable—the underlying economy is.

Ban issued → P2P trading volume rose +16% → underground market reached $17B annually by 2023 → ban reversed Dec 2023.

The difference is not the regulation. Nigeria has currency instability, large remittance flows, and 33.9 million financially excluded adults. Egypt does not.

Implications

What these findings mean for the people who use them.

For policymakers

Prohibition removes visibility, not demand.

When crypto is being driven by economic necessity, prohibition is the wrong tool. It pushes activity underground rather than reducing it—leaving regulators with less insight, not more control.

For businesses & investors

Grassroots demand follows failure, not clarity.

The strongest grassroots crypto markets are in countries where the financial system is failing—not in the most regulated jurisdictions. Read economic distress signals before reading regulatory league tables.

For researchers

Single-index rankings hide the mechanism.

Aggregate adoption rankings (like Chainalysis) systematically obscure the regulatory and economic context that explains why countries score the way they do. Disaggregated frameworks are necessary.
