Technical Infrastructure Methodology

How the Technical Infrastructure Score is calculated , pillar scores, normalization method, weighted geometric mean, and all data sources for 20 countries

Composite Formula

The Technical Infrastructure Score uses a weighted geometric mean across five per-capita pillars. Unlike an arithmetic mean, the geometric mean is non-compensatory: near-zero performance on any single pillar pulls the composite score toward zero, preventing a dominant pillar from masking weakness elsewhere.

Technical = BTC_Score0.25 × ETH_Score0.25 × Mining_Score0.15 × LN_Score0.20 × Enabling_Score0.15

Each pillar score = min(1, ln(raw_per_100k + 1) / ln(ceiling + 1)), yielding a continuous 0–1 value. Mining uses global hashrate % directly. All node/LN counts are per 100k internet users, not raw population, to fairly compare countries of different sizes.

All pillar scores are normalized 0–1 using the log-ceiling method described in the Normalization section. Scores are the product of all five pillars raised to their respective exponents (weighted geometric mean).

Each pillar measures a distinct aspect of blockchain network participation. BTC and ETH node density capture network decentralization; Lightning Network capacity signals payment infrastructure; Mining hashrate reflects network security contribution; Enabling Environment provides the foundational digital infrastructure gatekeeping variable.

Notable Patterns

  • US is the sole Network Leader (0.799): Hosts ~37.5% of global Bitcoin mining, plus the most BTC, ETH, and Lightning nodes in absolute terms. Only country where raw dominance survives per-capita normalization at scale.
  • UK ranks Advanced (0.470) despite near-zero mining: Its 0.013 mining score (just 0.05% of global hashrate) severely penalizes its composite under the geometric mean. Without the geometric mean penalty, it would rank far higher on node density alone.
  • India drops to Nascent (0.060): With 759M internet users as the denominator, even its sizeable node counts yield very low per-capita density. A reminder that scale cuts both ways in the per-capita model.
  • Ethiopia anomaly: Its 2.6% mining share (2nd highest in the sample after US) gives a mining pillar score of 0.345, but near-zero node density in every other pillar collapses the geometric mean to just 0.068 (Nascent). This illustrates the non-compensatory property of the geometric mean.
  • Indonesia similarly mined but node-sparse: Mining score 0.305 (from significant hashrate), but its BTC (0.049) and LN (0.041) pillar scores are among the lowest. Geometric mean composite: 0.092 (Emerging).
  • South Korea near-perfect Enabling (0.988): Highest internet penetration quality in the sample. However, mining score of 0.013 caps its composite at 0.303 (Developing).

Why the Geometric Mean?

A country that hosts many BTC nodes but has no mining presence provides a different kind of infrastructure than one with balanced participation. The geometric mean ensures all pillars must contribute meaningfully. Concretely:

  • If any single pillar = 0, the entire composite = 0
  • A pillar score of 0.010 (near-zero mining for most countries) acts as a 15% drag on the weighted exponent, pulling the composite down to at most ~0.93× of what a full mining score would yield
  • Countries that achieve balance across all five pillars outperform those with single-pillar dominance

Normalization Method

The log-ceiling normalization is used for all node and Lightning Network pillars. It compresses the extreme right-tail distribution of per-capita network participation while preserving meaningful differentiation at lower values.

Log-Ceiling Formula

Used for: BTC nodes, ETH nodes, Lightning Network

Formula: min(1, ln(x + 1) / ln(ceiling + 1))
Where x = raw per-100k-internet-users count. Countries exceeding the ceiling are capped at 1.0. Log transform compresses the long right tail (the US would dominate linearly).

Log-Ceiling (Mining)

Used for: Mining hashrate %

Formula: min(1, ln(hashrate% + 1) / ln(41))
Ceiling = 40% of global hashrate. Mining is entered as global share percentage, not per-capita, since hashrate is a network-level resource regardless of internet user base.

Weighted Average

Used for: Enabling Environment

Three sub-indicators are each min-max normalized within the 20-country sample, then combined: internet% × 0.40 + electricity% × 0.30 + speed_index × 0.30.

Why Per 100k Internet Users?

Denominator choice rationale

Internet users (not total population) is the correct denominator because node operators must have internet access. Using total population would unfairly penalize countries with low internet penetration twice , once in the enabling environment and again in node density.

Key Limitations

  • Node data is a snapshot: Bitnodes.io and Ethernodes.org measure reachable nodes at a point in time. Daily variance can be 5–10%. A 30-day average would be more robust.
  • Mining attribution is approximate: Hashrate Index country attribution is based on IP geolocation of mining pool connections, which may not accurately reflect physical location of mining hardware (VPN usage, data center diversity).
  • Lightning Network data: Only publicly announced LN nodes are counted. Private channels are a significant and growing portion of capacity, especially among merchant and institutional users.
  • Min-max sample dependency: All normalization is relative within the 20-country sample. Adding/removing a country would shift all scores.
  • Enabling environment speed index: Speedtest Global Index reflects consumer-grade speeds; enterprise and datacenter connectivity, more relevant for node operators, is not measured.
  • No Solana/other L1 nodes: The model focuses on BTC and ETH infrastructure as the two largest networks by market cap, security, and node count. Other L1 ecosystems are not captured.
