Global Blockchain
Adaptability Index Measuring blockchain readiness across economic, regulatory, and technical dimensions in 20 countries.

ALEXIS · ALON · ARI · FEDERICO · JADEN · NICO

01 · The Inquiry

How should global blockchain adoption be measured and visualized across economic, technical, and regulatory dimensions?

Most public reports rank countries on a single composite number. That hides the mechanism. A country can land at the top because of speculative trading, remittance flows, cheap mining electricity, or favorable laws, and those four stories require completely different responses from policymakers, businesses, and users.

Our project starts from the assumption that any single-number ranking is misleading, and asks what a more honest measurement looks like.

02 · Why this matters

Existing rankings answer the wrong question.

What others measure

  • Chainalysis: composite of on-chain activity, weighted toward CEX volume and DeFi flows.
  • Triple-A: a single ownership-percentage statistic per country.
  • Country reports (PwC, Library of Congress): legal status, no behavioral data.

What is missing

  • None of these separate why a country ranks high. India, Vietnam, and the US are all near the top of Chainalysis for completely different reasons.
  • None combine usage, legal clarity, and infrastructure into one comparable picture.
  • None reveal the tradeoff between regulation and adoption.
03 · Original thesis

High trading volume in a country would not correlate with strong technical infrastructure or regulatory clarity.

In other words: countries with the most crypto activity aren’t the ones with the best frameworks for it. Activity outruns regulation, and rule-making outruns the underlying network.

This is what we set out to test, and what we partly revised.

04 · Method

We split the score into three pillars, weighted equally.

Each country gets one score per pillar, normalized to 0–1. The overall score is their average. The point is to keep the pillars separable, so the underlying driver of any country’s position stays visible.

Economic Adoption

Weight 1/3

Real-world crypto use: ownership, exchange volume per GDP, remittances, Chainalysis ranks.

Regulatory Clarity

Weight 1/3

How clearly six legal dimensions are codified: stablecoins, licensing, tax, banking, enforcement, DeFi.

Technical Infrastructure

Weight 1/3

BTC and ETH nodes, mining hashrate, Lightning density, electricity, internet quality.

Overall = (Economic × 0.333) + (Regulatory × 0.333) + (Technical × 0.333)

06 · Pillar II · Regulatory Clarity

Six dimensions, each rated 1–3.

Built from Library of Congress and PwC primary sources. Each country is rated 1 (absent or hostile), 2 (partial), or 3 (clear and codified). The six ratings are summed and divided by 18 to normalize to 0–1. Equal weight on each dimension because no single dimension is sufficient on its own.

1/6
Stablecoin Law
Specific legislation governing stablecoin issuance, reserves, and consumer protection.
1/6
Exchange Licensing
A defined licensing regime for centralized exchanges and other VASPs.
1/6
Tax Treatment
Clear, codified rules for taxing crypto gains, income, and corporate holdings.
1/6
Banking Access
Whether crypto businesses can access traditional banking rails legally.
1/6
Enforcement
Active, observable enforcement of the rules that exist on paper.
1/6
DeFi Recognition
Whether DeFi protocols are acknowledged in the legal framework at all.
Regulatory = (Stablecoin + Licensing + Tax + Banking + Enforcement + DeFi) / 18

10 · Finding I · The Conditional Relationship

Same restriction, opposite outcomes.

When we plot Regulatory Clarity against Economic Adoption, four distinct country types appear. Restriction does not have a single, universal effect.

4
Distinct country profiles emerge once the score is split into pillars instead of averaged.

Restrictive policy fails to suppress adoption wherever any of four conditions is present: currency instability, large remittance flows, financial exclusion, or sanctions and conflict.

11 · Finding II · The Japan Paradox

Comprehensive regulation does not produce adoption when there is no economic need.

Japan is the single sharpest piece of evidence that “more regulation = more adoption” is wrong.

Regulatory Clarity
0.944 · #1 of 20

Highest score in the index. The only country in the dataset to reach a 3 (clear and codified) on stablecoin legislation.

Economic Adoption
0.205 · #18 of 20

Lower than Bangladesh, Yemen, and the UK. Few citizens own crypto, exchange volume is modest relative to GDP.

Clear rules without economic demand produce a regulated framework that few people actually use. Japan is the existence proof of one of the four quadrants on the previous slide.

Crypto’s “decentralized” layers are narrowly held.

countries in the index reach a 3 on DeFi recognition. Even “Comprehensive”-tier regulators have not caught up.

Infrastructure concentration is a geopolitical question, not just a technical one. DeFi and stablecoins are the next regulatory frontier everywhere.

13 · Revised thesis

High crypto activity does not mean a country is truly blockchain-ready.

Some countries are active out of necessity. Others have built real infrastructure. You cannot tell the difference without looking at all three dimensions separately.

What the original thesis got right

  • High trading volume does not reliably correlate with regulatory clarity.
  • Activity outruns rules in most distressed economies (Vietnam, Nigeria, Argentina).

What we revised

  • Economic adoption and technical infrastructure are not correlated at all: r = ~0.2 across our 20 countries.
  • Countries reach “blockchain-ready” through completely different paths. The composite hides which path.
14 · Relevance

Different audiences, different reads.

The split-pillar score is most useful when it changes a decision. A few examples we identified.

Policymakers

Read

Prohibition removes visibility, not demand, when economic conditions are driving usage. Channel through licensing instead.

Investors / Analysts

Read

Distress signals (remittances, currency stress) predict adoption better than legal-clarity rankings.

Founders

Read

Match the product to the country’s underlying problem. Stablecoin rails fit Vietnam; compliance products fit Japan.

Researchers

Read

Aggregate adoption rankings systematically obscure mechanism. Disaggregated frameworks are required to make causal claims.

Each of the six audiences on the artifact’s findings page has a one-paragraph implication tied directly to a measurable variable.

15 · Limitations & what comes next

Honest limits of the index.

Limits

  • 20-country sample chosen for data availability and ecosystem relevance, not statistical representativeness.
  • Equal pillar weights are a defensible choice, not a derived one. Different weights would produce different orderings near the middle.
  • Snapshot at March 2026; Chainalysis data and regulatory ratings shift faster than this.

What comes next

  • Add 10–15 more countries (notably China, despite policy ambiguity).
  • Year-over-year deltas to track which pillar a country’s score is moving on.
  • Couple the legal ratings to enforcement actions data so “Comprehensive” doesn’t mean “on paper only.”
