Blockchain
Adoption Atlas Measuring blockchain readiness across economic, regulatory, and technical dimensions in 20 countries.

FEDERICO BECERRA · NICO TAYLOR · JADEN CHOI · ALEXIS VO · ALON MUTTER · ARI CHOE

Presented by ALON / ARI / ALEXIS
01 · The Problem

Existing rankings answer the wrong question.

Current blockchain adoption metrics are fragmented and conflate speculation with genuine utility.

What others measure

  • Chainalysis: composite of on-chain activity, weighted toward CEX volume and DeFi flows.
  • Triple-A: a single ownership-percentage statistic per country.
  • Country reports (PwC, Library of Congress): legal status, no behavioral data.

What is missing

  • None separate why a country ranks high. India, Vietnam, and the US sit near the top of Chainalysis for completely different reasons.
  • None combine usage, legal clarity, and infrastructure into one comparable picture.
  • None reveal the tradeoff between regulation and adoption.
Presented by ALON / ARI / ALEXIS
02 · Our Question

How should global blockchain adoption be measured and visualized across economic, technical, and regulatory dimensions?

A defensible answer requires keeping the three dimensions separable, not collapsing them into a single number that hides which one is doing the work.

Presented by ALON / ARI / ALEXIS
03 · Our Thesis

What we expected vs. what we found.

Original thesis
High trading volume would not correlate with strong technical infrastructure or regulatory clarity.

i.e., countries with the most activity aren’t the ones with the best frameworks for it.

Revised thesis
High crypto activity does not mean a country is truly blockchain-ready.

Some countries are active out of necessity, others have built real infrastructure, and you can’t tell the difference without looking at all three dimensions separately.

The original thesis was partially confirmed. The revised thesis is what the data actually showed.

Presented by ALON / ARI / ALEXIS
04 · Method

Three pillars, weighted equally.

Each country gets one score per pillar, normalized to 0–1. The overall score is their average. The point is to keep the pillars separable, so the underlying driver of any country’s position stays visible.

Economic Adoption

Weight 1/3

Real-world crypto use: ownership, exchange volume per GDP, remittances, Chainalysis ranks.

Regulatory Clarity

Weight 1/3

Six legal sub-dimensions: stablecoins, licensing, tax, banking, enforcement, DeFi.

Technical Infrastructure

Weight 1/3

BTC and ETH nodes, mining hashrate, Lightning density, electricity, internet quality.

Overall = (Economic × 0.333) + (Regulatory × 0.333) + (Technical × 0.333)
Presented by NICO TAYLOR
05 · Pillar I · Economic Dimension

How much economic value is flowing through blockchain, and is it speculation or productive use?

30%
Crypto Ownership
Population penetration rate from Triple-A. The strongest signal of real consumer adoption.
20%
CEX Activity
Average of Chainalysis Retail and Institutional CEX sub-rankings, PPP-adjusted. Captures broad market participation.
20%
CEX Volume / GDP
CoinGecko 24h exchange volume divided by national GDP, log-adjusted. Catches Lira, Won, and Naira-style speculation.
20%
Remittances per Capita
World Bank inbound flows divided by population. Indicates real-world cross-border utility (Ukraine, Philippines, Bangladesh).
10%
DeFi Activity
Chainalysis DeFi value-received sub-rank. Down-weighted because it is volatile and concentrated in a small group of countries.
Presented by FEDERICO BECERRA
06 · Pillar II · Technical Infrastructure

How is blockchain infrastructure being built and where?

25%
Bitcoin Nodes
Reachable BTC nodes per 100k internet users (Bitnodes).
25%
Ethereum Nodes
Reachable ETH nodes per 100k internet users (Ethernodes).
20%
Lightning Network
LN nodes per 100k internet users (mempool.emzy.de). A proxy for second-layer adoption depth.
15%
Mining Hashrate
Country share of global BTC hashrate (Hashrate Index). Used directly because mining is geographically concentrated.
15%
Enabling Environment
Composite of internet penetration (40%), electricity access (30%), and average internet speed (30%).
Presented by FEDERICO BECERRA
07 · Why this technical weighting

The reasoning behind the five technical indicators.

  • Balances decentralization and economic commitment. Nodes (BTC / ETH / LN) measure decentralization and participation, while hashrate captures capital-intensive commitment to the network.
  • Per-100k-internet-users normalization improves cross-country comparability. Makes small but highly engaged countries comparable to large economies, instead of penalizing them for population size.
  • Incorporates real-world infrastructure constraints. Crypto infrastructure cannot scale without underlying connectivity and energy systems, which is what the enabling-environment factor captures.
  • Weights reflect ecosystem importance. Prioritizes core protocol infrastructure (BTC and ETH at 25% each) but also recognizes scalability and security layers (Lightning at 20%, Mining at 15%).
Presented by ALON / ARI / ALEXIS
08 · Pillar III · Regulatory Dimension

How clear, comprehensive, and enforceable is each country’s framework?

Each country is rated on six sub-dimensions, scored 1 (absent or hostile), 2 (partial), or 3 (clear and codified). The six ratings sum and divide by 18 to normalize to 0–1. Equal weight on each because no single dimension is sufficient on its own.

Exchange Legality

Whether centralized exchanges can operate legally at all in the jurisdiction.

Licensing Framework (VASP)

Defined licensing regime for centralized exchanges and other Virtual Asset Service Providers.

Stablecoin Regulation

Specific legislation governing stablecoin issuance, reserves, and consumer protection.

Tax Clarity

Codified rules for taxing crypto gains, income, and corporate holdings.

Banking System Access

Whether crypto businesses can access traditional banking rails legally.

Enforcement & Stance

Active, observable enforcement of the rules that exist on paper, plus government posture.

Presented by ALON / ARI / ALEXIS
09 · Score Interpretation

Higher scores reflect clarity and usability, not strictness.

0.8 – 1.0
Comprehensive

Clear legal frameworks across all categories. Businesses and users can operate with high certainty.

0.6 – 0.8
Developing

Partial regulation with some gaps. Key areas defined, but inconsistencies remain.

0.4 – 0.6
Ambiguous

Unclear or fragmented rules. High uncertainty for users and companies.

0.2 – 0.4
Hostile

Restrictive or prohibitive policies. Limited or no legal pathway for participation.

Five of 20 countries reach Comprehensive: Japan (0.944), Ukraine (0.889), Brazil (0.889), Thailand (0.833), United Kingdom (0.833).

Presented by JADEN CHOI

PART TWO

Key Findings

Six insights from analyzing 20 countries across the three dimensions.

Presented by ALON MUTTER
12 · Finding I · Adoption vs. Regulation

An inverse relationship, with two notable exceptions.

Heavy regulation slows consumer-led adoption. Light regulation enables rapid but potentially risky growth.

The exceptions
Ukraine & Brazil

The only countries achieving both high adoption (#2 and #11 economic) and Comprehensive regulation (0.889 each). Both reached this through proactive frameworks responding to existing demand.

The pattern
High adoption, low clarity

Vietnam (0.761 econ / 0.556 reg), Pakistan (0.494 / 0.556), Argentina (0.262 / 0.611). Activity outruns rules wherever economic necessity is the driver.

The takeaway. The ideal balance, high adoption with clear rules, remains rare globally. Most countries pick one or the other.

Presented by ARI CHOE
13 · Finding II · Developing Nations Lead

Every top-five adopter has a distress driver.

The leaders look nothing like a list of “most regulated” countries. The US is the only stable, developed economy in the top five.

#1
Vietnam

0.761

Ukraine
Active conflict, currency stress, $503/capita remittances (highest in index)
0.733
#3
Philippines
Major remittance corridor ($341/capita), 13.4% ownership
0.632
#4
United States
Outlier: 15.6% ownership, $76.6B daily CEX volume, deep institutional flows
0.576
#5
Pakistan

0.494

Three structural drivers recur: currency instability (Venezuela 10.3% / Argentina 9.7% ownership), remittance corridors, and financial exclusion, all more powerful predictors than favorable policy.

14 · Finding III · The Regulatory Landscape

Only 5 of 20 countries have comprehensive regulation.

5/20
Comprehensive only. Japan (0.944), Ukraine (0.889), Brazil (0.889), Thailand (0.833), UK (0.833). The remaining 15 are Developing, Ambiguous, or Hostile.
0/20
DeFi recognition. No country in the index scores a 3 on DeFi recognition. Traditional legal frameworks cannot classify decentralized protocols.
3
Hostile-tier countries. Bangladesh, Yemen, and Ethiopia all score 0.333 across all six sub-dimensions. Operating there carries maximum legal risk.

The takeaway. Even the most regulated jurisdictions still have a global blind spot: stablecoin and DeFi rules. That is the next regulatory frontier everywhere.

Presented by JADEN CHOI
15 · Finding IV · The Same Country, Three Stories

Rank by adoption alone, and you miss the country.

In this index, the same country routinely ranks near the top on one pillar and near the bottom on another. Average those into one number and the actual story disappears.

Rules without use
Japan
Regulatory#1 Technical#4 Economic#18

World-class rules, strong infrastructure, and almost no consumer use. Heavy regulation appears to throttle adoption rather than direct it.

Use without rules
Vietnam
Economic#1 Technical#11 Regulatory#15

Highest crypto ownership in the world (21.2%), driven by remittances and currency stress. The rules and infrastructure have not caught up to demand.

Framework without demand
United Kingdom
Technical#3 Regulatory#4 Economic#17

Comprehensive regulation and the highest per-capita node density in the index, yet usage trails most developing economies. The framework alone does not create users.

Demand without infrastructure
Pakistan
Economic#5 Regulatory#15 Technical#17

Top-3 retail and institutional CEX activity from financial exclusion and remittance flows, with no regulatory framework or technical infrastructure underneath.

A single composite rank would average these four countries into roughly the same middle bucket. The split-pillar score keeps the actual story visible: who has rules, who has users, who has infrastructure, and which pillar is missing.

Presented by NICO TAYLOR
16 · Finding V · Crisis Drives Adoption

Sanctions and economic crisis are stronger catalysts than policy.

Restrictive rules alone cannot prevent adoption when citizens face economic necessity.

Russia
Sanctions, regulation labeled “Developing” (0.611)

Yet 16.4% of global Bitcoin hashrate (#2 worldwide) and an “Advanced”-tier technical infrastructure score (0.584). International isolation has paradoxically deepened the country’s crypto presence.

Venezuela
Ambiguous regulation (0.556), hyperinflation

10.3% crypto ownership, higher than Japan, South Korea, or the United Kingdom. USDT is functionally a parallel dollar; restrictive policy hasn’t slowed it.

For policymakers. Channel adoption through clear frameworks rather than attempting to block it. Bans push usage into P2P and self-custody, where regulators have less visibility, not more control.

Presented by FEDERICO BECERRA
17 · Finding VI · Market Entry Recommendations

Five distinct risk-reward profiles.

The split-pillar score lets us group countries by the kind of opportunity (and risk) they actually offer.

Low Risk

Proven Markets

US, UK, Japan, Brazil

Legal certainty + strong infrastructure. Best for regulated exchanges and institutional / enterprise products.

High Growth

Developing Regulation

Ukraine, India, Philippines, Indonesia, Thailand

Large user bases with regulation moving in the right direction. Ideal for wallets, remittance, DeFi, P2P.

High Risk

Necessity-Driven

Vietnam, Nigeria, Pakistan, Argentina, Venezuela

Massive necessity-driven adoption. Regulatory standing can shift rapidly. High reward, high downside.

Infrastructure

Mining / Node Plays

Russia, Ethiopia, Indonesia

Mining and node infrastructure opportunities driven by cheap energy, regardless of regulatory environment.

Avoid

No Viable Path

Bangladesh, Yemen

Hostile regulation plus minimal infrastructure. No viable entry point currently.

Presented by FEDERICO BECERRA
18 · Final Conclusion · Who Should Act

Different audiences, different reads.

The split-pillar score is most useful when it changes a decision. Five audiences, five concrete reads.

Business Executives

Identify entry markets and allocate resources with legal certainty.

Use the risk-reward profiles to match the product to the country’s underlying problem.

Lawyers & Compliance

Pinpoint legal risk by jurisdiction.

Sub-dimension breakdown (stablecoin, licensing, tax, DeFi) makes risk legible at a granular level.

Policymakers

Pursue balance, not restriction.

Ukraine and Brazil prove comprehensive regulation and high adoption can coexist. Bans push usage out of view.

Investors & VCs

Read the trajectory, not just the level.

Adoption + improving regulation signals the highest-potential markets. Infrastructure scores reveal where the network is being built.

Blockchain Developers

Where to build infrastructure next.

Node and validator concentration data guides network expansion, validator location selection, and underserved-region targeting.

One takeaway. No single number captures blockchain readiness. The split-pillar approach is the contribution: it makes the underlying driver of every country’s position legible to whoever needs to act on it.
