Composite Formula

The Economic Adoption Score combines seven normalized indicators, each weighted by how directly it reflects genuine crypto economic activity:

Score = (CEX Activity × 0.15) + (DeFi × 0.10) + (Ownership × 0.25) + (Remittances × 0.15) + (Currency Instability × 0.10) + (CEX/GDP × 0.15) + (Internet × 0.10)

Why Decompose?

  • Our own weighting: Version E used Chainalysis's composite rank (geometric mean of all sub-indexes). This meant our model partially imported their weighting decisions. Decomposing lets us weight CEX vs DeFi activity ourselves.
  • Surface hidden signals: Countries like Ethiopia (#7 DeFi) and Nigeria (#3 DeFi) show strong decentralized activity that gets diluted in Chainalysis's composite. Our decomposition surfaces this.
  • Avoid double-counting: Our CEX Volume/GDP metric uses CoinGecko data (raw exchange volume), while Chainalysis measures PPP-weighted on-chain flows. Pearson correlation between them is 0.382 — well below the 0.7 concern threshold.

2025 Methodology Changes (by Chainalysis)

  • Dropped: Retail DeFi sub-index (skewed by low-frequency behavior)
  • Added: Institutional CEX sub-index (captures ETF-era flows)
  • Net effect: More weight on centralized + institutional activity

Normalization Methods

Every indicator uses different units — ranks, percentages, dollars, ratios. You cannot add "rank #3" to "21% ownership." Normalization converts everything to a 0–1 scale so indicators are comparable and can be combined into a weighted composite score.

1. Standard Min-Max

Used for: Ownership %, Remittances/Capita, Internet %

Formula: (x − min) / (max − min)
Lowest value in sample = 0, highest = 1. Everything else falls proportionally between them. Simple, intuitive, preserves proportional distance.

2. Inverted Min-Max

Used for: CEX Activity Rank, DeFi Rank

Formula: (max − x) / (max − min)
Same as standard but flipped — because for ranks, lower is better. Without inverting, rank #1 (India) would score lowest. After inverting: rank #1 = highest score.

3. Log Transform → Min-Max

Used for: Inflation, Depreciation, CEX Vol/GDP

First take LN(value), then apply standard min-max. Extreme outliers (Venezuela's 618% inflation) would crush everything else without log transform. Log compresses extremes while preserving rank order.

4. Composite Average

Used for: Currency Instability (3 sub-indicators)

Three signals — log(inflation), depreciation, capital controls — each normalized individually (0–1), then averaged. Captures why people need a crypto hedge, not just one symptom.

5. Arithmetic Average of Ranks

Used for: CEX Activity (Retail + Inst. CEX)

Retail CEX rank and Institutional CEX rank are averaged before normalization. Rewards balanced activity across both grassroots and institutional flows.

Key Limitations

Transparent acknowledgment of model constraints. These are known limitations, not oversights.

  • Ownership data fragility: Triple-A survey data carries the highest weight (25%) but has unpublished confidence intervals. Sensitivity analysis shows rankings remain directionally stable at ±30% variation.
  • CEX volume is a snapshot: CoinGecko 24h volume is a single day's reading (March 2026). A 30–90 day average would be more robust but is unavailable via the free-tier API.
  • Mixed data vintages: Indicators span different periods — Chainalysis 2025, Ownership 2024, Inflation Feb/Mar 2026, CEX volume live, GDP 2024.
  • Min-max sample dependency: Scores are normalized within only 20 countries. Adding or removing a country would shift all normalized values.
  • Chainalysis ranks are ordinal: We treat ranks as cardinal (equal intervals). Underlying cardinal values require an enterprise license.
  • No P2P or stablecoin data: Important for Nigeria, Venezuela, and Ethiopia where informal channels dominate, but no public country-level data available.

This model prioritizes transparency over precision. Every limitation above is a conscious design choice driven by data availability constraints in a non-funded academic project.
